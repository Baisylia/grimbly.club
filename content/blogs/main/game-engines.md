# Game Engines

Since 2021 Unreal Engine has been my main engine of choice when creating games, as I used both it and Unity throughout high school and found the latter to be a lot more... terrible in every way than the other. The last thing I used UE for was to create the prototype of Grimbly Garden for my final university project, as it was the required engine to my delight, but I've had a change of heart of sorts this year.

I have been away from my computer for a long time, both by being bedridden and by being stuck outside of Australia waiting for raspmary to be allowed in, which made development on Grimbly Garden near-impossible. I tried installing it on my laptop to use in bed in Turkey, but ended up having storage issues and a lot of lag. This, combined with the weight added onto a game by it simply being made in UE, has led me to a decision. Grimbly Garden is not going be made in Unreal Engine.

While I never used it for any official projects, there is one more engine that I have some experience in, and that's Godot. It's light, open source, and doesn't take up all of my laptop's storage. This is the new engine being used for Grimbly Garden going forward.

I've made a decent amount of progress on the game since the switch, as I've actually been able to do work without my computer, and I must say I've really been enjoying the engine. I've seen some people doubt the engine, as it is relatively new and has been lacking in certain features in the past, but in its current state I think it's really good, and perfect for a small indie game such as Grimbly Garden.

If you're looking for an engine to create a game in, I would still recommend UE for any large projects, or anything requiring complex shaders and lighting, but overall Godot is my new top choice. Give it a try!"
